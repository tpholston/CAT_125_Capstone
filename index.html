About

Survivability

In Quebec from 1998-2008, the 10-year survival rate was 44% for cooperatives and 19.5% for other enterprises

In Quebec from 1989-1998, the 10-year survival was 46% for cooperatives and 20% for non-cooperatives

Efficiency and Productivity

Although some may argue that a lack of incentivization can lead to lower efficiency levels, this isn't supported in research. One study (Craig and Pencavel) found that cooperatives may raise productivity by 6 to 14 percent but no definitive conclusions could be made from this due to a couple factors detailed on page 167. However another more recent study on several thousand firms from France found cooperative productivity to be the same as or even higher than their conventional counterparts.

Good Sustainable Jobs in the Community

Worker co-ops have been shown to choose employment stability over income stability. Employee members are able to choose to preserve jobs in which they have a say in determining working conditions and employment risk, and in which they are likely to be better motivated, more creative, and happier. The cooperatives may affect public finances and health positively by decreasing unemployment risk both in the cooperatives themselves and in the community.

Perverse Supply Response

Worker cooperatives have shown that they will consistently cut output and jobs in response to an increase in the price of its product or service. Major studies looking at worker cooperatives from different traditions in three different countries in comparison with conventional firms have recently found that the cooperatives do not respond perversely to demand shocks.

If they are so successful, then where are they?

The major critique I hear from proponents of conventional business (particularly David Friedman in this video) is this one and it seems logical enough: "In the market businesses compete to survive and grow. If cooperatives are so successful, then why aren't they everywhere? Why aren't they dominating the market?". This is a really good question to ask but there are a couple things wrong. Let's start by tackling the underlying presupposition which is that growth and size is a good metric of success. Endless pursuit of growth isn't necessarily something we should be striving for as a society and I think the climate crisis is the best argument in support of this. In an economy that promotes growth and profitability over the wellbeing of people we have seen and will continue to see the ramifications of rampant externalities. Although a worker cooperative doesn't necessarily tackle this externality issue, it does provide companies that put profitability secondary to the needs of workers. In the case of one of the largest worker cooperatives in the world, Mondragon, during a major economic downturn in the Spanish economy they demonstrated a deep commitment to the continuation of employment for its members. This is just one example. Many studies have demonstrated that while conventional firms tend to adjust employment during periods of economic turmoil, cooperatives tend to adjust pay more than employment ("Good Sustainable Jobs in the Community" link). So, while we may not see many Google or Amazon sized cooperatives, maybe that isn't the metric we should be going off of. Now even if you're not convinced by that, maybe you don't believe in climate change, or you just don't care, the reality is that most corporations are very small. Among the firms that have at least one employee, around 90 per cent have less than 20 employees in the UK (93.7 percent) the US (89.6 percent) and France (90.4 percent) and less than 10 employees in Spain. Large firms are very rare. Only 0.3 percent of the firms that have employees have 500 employees or more in the UK and the US, 0.2 percent in France and 0.1 percent in Spain. Although we always hear about the fortune 500 companies, the reality is most companies are very small. If you really want to see a cooperative that seems to be really dominating the market though, navigate to the section of this site on Mondragon, the seventh largest corporation in Spain. This is an extremely successful and big corporation that employs thousands. In addition, there are many obstacles for cooperatives that conventional businesses don't necessarily have to worry about. Things like initial investment can be really hard for cooperatives to garnish as a result of liability. Cooperatives don't have one person who is responsible for investment and as a result, getting loans from banks can be a difficult hurdle to get over. Gathering the initial capital to start a business is tough but through political action (or even social action in making these types of unconventional investments more socially acceptable) we can one day hope to create easier paths for cooperatives to get off the ground. After all, it seems this would be a really good investment of funds, as the turnover rate for cooperatives is significantly lower than conventional corporations.

Mondragon


Mondragon Corporation is the world's largest worker cooperative as well as the seventh largest company in Spain. The corporation was founded by a priest named José María Arizmendiarrieta along with five other workers in 1956. When the founders moved to the Mondragon region, it had been neglected for nearly one hundred years. There were no employment opportunities, very poor education, and little hope for the future. As a result, the cooperative has maintained in it’s core values the importance of grassroot management and investment in community action and education.

Mondragon is a collection of worker co-ops where members democratically and collectively own the corporation. The process behind acquiring new members is done through probationary periods. A prospective member will be given a period of time where they are overseen by their peers. Subsequently, the prospective member’s peers will make a democratic decision alongside the elected board of directors of mondragon to determine whether or not this person will become a member. When a person becomes a member they have to pay a membership fee to the corporation and are given a vote in what is very similar to a conventional corporation’s shareholder meeting. Votes are done to determine rules, policies, and who the company's board of directors are. In this way workers play a direct role in not only production but deciding what to do with company profits.

The company is highly competitive and is in many markets around the globe employing over 80,000 people across 37 countries. Mondragon maintains that the key to it’s massive success is in large part a result of the participatory management system. Workers have direct say in the operations of the business and what to do with company profits and as a result these profits tend to go towards increasing pay and benefits. Cooperatives like Mondragon tend to make less risky investments and as a result continue to be beacons of long lasting and stable employment for its members. In a 2008 recession, the Spanish economy was shrinking at an annual rate of 1.9%. As a result, Mondragon democratically decided rather than laying workers off like many conventional businesses were doing they would implement a worker income drop between 5-10%. Unlike other companies where the majority of pay cuts go towards laying off workers while managerial pay tends to soar, Mondragon’s managers took the biggest cuts. The wage cuts were implemented in 2009 when most other Spanish companies were firing workers to reduce costs (a total of 3.5 million people were added to the unemployed during this time).

Worker’s enjoy benefits including paid maternity and paternity leave alongside free higher education opportunities including their own non-profit university (Mondragon University). Workers have consistently seen higher wages in comparison to their conventional counterparts in Spain. Another value of mondragon is that of corporate social responsibility to fair distribution of wealth. One of the interesting policies that worker cooperatives like Mondragon have historically implemented is the idea of a cap on lowest to highest associate pay. No manager within any cooperative can make more than six times the salary of any worker in the cooperative. CEO compensation is hundreds of times greater than that of their average employee. In a world where income inequality in conventional enterprises iis such a big issue, perhaps following models like Mondragon can begin to curb these massive inequities.
